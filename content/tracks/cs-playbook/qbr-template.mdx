## 📊 QBR Template & Executive Reviews

The Quarterly Business Review is the single most important touchpoint in a CSM's calendar. A well-executed QBR reinforces value, aligns on strategy, uncovers expansion opportunities, and builds executive relationships. A poorly executed one accelerates churn. This guide provides battle-tested frameworks, presentation templates, and tactical advice to ensure every QBR moves the account forward.

### QBR Preparation Checklist

Preparation is where QBRs are won or lost. Rushing to build a deck the night before guarantees a mediocre review. Follow this phased timeline to ensure you walk in fully prepared.

<Expandable title="T-14 Days: Data Collection & Stakeholder Alignment" defaultOpen={true}>

**Data gathering:**
- Pull platform usage metrics for the full quarter: daily active users, audiences created, destinations synced, data ingestion volumes
- Export identity resolution match rates and trend data from the Identity+ dashboard
- Collect Ada AI usage statistics: queries run, insights generated, actions taken
- Gather support ticket summary: total tickets, average resolution time, open issues, CSAT scores
- Review all CRM notes from the quarter: meeting notes, email threads, escalation history

**Stakeholder alignment:**
- Confirm QBR attendees with your champion. Push for executive sponsor attendance
- Send a pre-QBR survey to the customer asking: What went well this quarter? What could improve? What are your priorities for next quarter?
- Schedule a 30-minute internal prep meeting with your manager and solutions consultant
- Identify any sensitive topics (budget pressure, competitive evaluation, unresolved escalations) and plan your approach

**Account context:**
- Review the current health score and note any dimension-level changes
- Check renewal date and contract terms. If renewal is within 180 days, adjust the QBR to include renewal themes
- Review the success plan: which use cases were planned? Which are delivered? Which are stalled?
- Identify 2-3 expansion opportunities to weave into the conversation naturally

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="T-7 Days: Deck Build & Internal Review" defaultOpen={false}>

**Deck construction:**
- Build the QBR deck using the standard template (see agenda section below)
- Populate all data slides with actual metrics. Never present placeholder data
- Create comparison charts: this quarter vs. last quarter vs. account benchmarks
- Prepare 2-3 strategic recommendations with clear business justifications
- Draft next quarter goals with measurable success criteria

**Internal review:**
- Walk through the deck with your manager. Focus on the narrative, not just the data
- Review strategic recommendations with your solutions consultant for technical feasibility
- If expansion will be discussed, align with your sales counterpart on positioning and pricing
- Rehearse your response to anticipated tough questions

**Logistics:**
- Send a calendar invite with agenda, attendee list, and any pre-read materials
- Confirm AV setup or video conferencing details
- Prepare a backup plan if key stakeholders cancel last minute

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="T-1 Day: Final Preparation" defaultOpen={false}>

**Final checks:**
- Review the deck one final time. Check for typos, stale data, and formatting issues
- Verify all metrics are current as of the most recent data refresh
- Prepare printed copies or a shareable PDF version of the deck
- Review attendee LinkedIn profiles and recent company news for conversation starters
- Prepare your opening statement: set the tone in the first 60 seconds

**Mindset preparation:**
- Identify your three key outcomes for the meeting. What must you leave with?
- Anticipate the two hardest questions you might face and rehearse answers
- Remind yourself: the QBR is about the customer's success, not a product demo

</Expandable>

<Callout type="tip" title="The Pre-QBR Survey is Non-Negotiable">
Sending a brief 3-5 question survey two weeks before the QBR accomplishes three things: it signals that you care about their perspective, it surfaces issues you might not know about, and it gives you time to prepare thoughtful responses instead of being blindsided in the meeting. Even if only one person responds, the insights are invaluable.
</Callout>

### Section 1: Business Outcomes Review (10 minutes)

This is the most important section of the QBR. Lead with business impact, not platform activity. Executives care about what the platform did for their business, not how many audiences were created.

**Structure your outcomes review around these questions:**
1. What were the agreed goals at the start of the quarter?
2. Which goals were achieved, partially achieved, or missed?
3. What measurable business impact was delivered?

The most powerful QBR moments happen when the customer presents their own results. Before the meeting, ask your champion if they would be willing to share a specific win. When the customer articulates the value in their own words, it is far more credible and memorable than a CSM presenting the same data.

### Section 2: Platform Adoption Metrics (10 minutes)

Present adoption data as a narrative, not a data dump. Each metric should answer the question: "So what?"

**Key metrics to present:**

**How to present the data effectively:**

- **Show trends, not snapshots.** A match rate of 72% means nothing without context. A match rate that grew from 58% to 72% over the quarter tells a compelling story
- **Benchmark against peers.** "Your identity resolution rate of 72% puts you in the top quartile of retail CDP customers" is more impactful than the number alone
- **Connect metrics to outcomes.** "The increase in active destinations from 3 to 7 means your audiences are now reaching more channels, which contributed to the 15% lift in campaign reach"

### Section 3: Support Review (5 minutes)

Keep the support section concise. Executives do not want a ticket-by-ticket walkthrough. They want confidence that issues are handled well.

<StatCard stats={[
  { value: "Total Tickets", label: "Volume This Quarter", color: "#3b82f6" },
  { value: "Avg Resolution", label: "Time to Resolve", color: "#10b981" },
  { value: "CSAT Score", label: "Satisfaction Rating", color: "#a855f7" },
  { value: "Open Issues", label: "Currently Unresolved", color: "#f59e0b" }
]} />

**Present support data in three parts:**
1. **Summary statistics:** Total tickets, resolution time, CSAT. Compare to last quarter
2. **Notable incidents:** Briefly cover any P1/P2 issues, root cause, and resolution. Focus on what was learned and what changed
3. **Open items:** List any unresolved issues with expected resolution dates. Never surprise an executive with an open issue they did not know about

<Callout type="warning" title="Own the Negatives">
If there were support failures this quarter, address them head-on. Do not hide bad data or skip the support slide. Acknowledge the issue, explain the root cause, describe the fix, and share what changed to prevent recurrence. Executives respect transparency far more than avoidance.
</Callout>

### Section 4: Roadmap Alignment (10 minutes)

Roadmap alignment is where you demonstrate that Zeotap is investing in the customer's future, not just serving their present needs.

**Structure:**
1. **Features delivered this quarter** that are relevant to the customer's use cases
2. **Upcoming features** on the next quarter roadmap that align with their priorities
3. **Customer feature requests:** status update on any requests they have submitted
4. **Industry trends** that inform Zeotap's product direction

<Callout type="important" title="Never Over-Promise on Roadmap">
Share only features that are committed for the upcoming quarter. Do not share tentative plans as certainties. Use language like "We are actively working on..." or "This is on our roadmap for the second half of the year" rather than "This will be available next month." Broken roadmap promises erode trust faster than almost anything else.
</Callout>

---

### Section 5: Strategic Recommendations (10 minutes)

This section is where you earn your seat at the table. Strategic recommendations demonstrate that you understand the customer's business, not just their platform usage.

**Every recommendation must follow this structure:**
1. **Observation:** What did you see in the data or hear from the team?
2. **Insight:** Why does it matter for their business?
3. **Recommendation:** What specific action should they take?
4. **Expected outcome:** What result can they expect?

<Expandable title="Example Recommendation: Expand Identity Resolution Coverage" defaultOpen={true}>

**Observation:** Your current identity resolution match rate is 68%, which means roughly one-third of your customer interactions are not being connected to unified profiles.

**Insight:** Unresolved profiles cannot be activated in downstream destinations, which limits campaign reach and personalization effectiveness. For a retailer processing 2M events daily, this represents approximately 640K daily interactions that are not contributing to audience segments.

**Recommendation:** Integrate your mobile app SDK and loyalty program data as additional identity signals into the Zeotap identity graph. Based on similar implementations, this typically increases match rates by 12-18 percentage points.

**Expected outcome:** Improving match rates from 68% to 82% would increase your addressable audience by approximately 400K profiles, directly expanding campaign reach and enabling cross-device personalization.

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Example Recommendation: Activate Ada AI for Self-Service Insights" defaultOpen={false}>

**Observation:** Your team currently creates audience segments manually using the Audience Builder, averaging 45 minutes per segment. Ada AI usage is at zero this quarter.

**Insight:** Manual segment creation limits the number of campaigns your team can run and creates a bottleneck around your two power users. Teams using Ada AI for natural language audience creation report 70% faster segment creation times.

**Recommendation:** Schedule a 60-minute Ada AI enablement session with your marketing team. Start with three high-frequency segment types that your team builds repeatedly.

**Expected outcome:** Reducing segment creation time from 45 minutes to 15 minutes per segment would free up approximately 10 hours per month of analyst time, enabling your team to run more campaigns without adding headcount.

</Expandable>

### Handling Difficult QBRs

Not every QBR is a celebration. Some of the most important QBRs happen when things are not going well. How you handle adversity defines the customer relationship.

<Expandable title="Scenario: Declining Usage" defaultOpen={true}>

**Signals:** WAU dropping, fewer audiences created, feature breadth narrowing, login frequency declining.

**Approach:**
1. **Acknowledge it directly.** Open with: "We have noticed a decline in platform activity this quarter, and we want to understand why so we can help."
2. **Listen before prescribing.** The decline might be due to internal reorganization, budget cuts, seasonal patterns, or a strategic shift. Ask open-ended questions
3. **Avoid being defensive.** Do not immediately jump to "here are all the features you are not using." That feels like a guilt trip
4. **Propose a re-engagement plan.** Offer a structured 30-day plan: refresher training, new use case workshop, dedicated SE support
5. **Escalate internally.** Brief your manager and update the health score immediately after the QBR

**Key phrase:** "Our goal is to make sure you are getting maximum value from your investment. Let us work together to identify what is getting in the way."

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Scenario: Active Escalation" defaultOpen={false}>

**Signals:** Unresolved P1/P2 tickets, frustrated stakeholders, executive complaints, formal escalation filed.

**Approach:**
1. **Lead the QBR with the escalation.** Do not bury it at the end. Address it in the first 5 minutes
2. **Present a clear timeline:** What happened, when, what was done, current status, and path to resolution
3. **Bring reinforcements.** Have your engineering or product leader join the call to demonstrate seriousness
4. **Own the failure.** Even if the root cause was partially on the customer's side, lead with what Zeotap could have done better
5. **Offer concrete remediation.** This might include dedicated support hours, SLA credits, or an accelerated fix timeline

**Key phrase:** "We take this seriously, and I want to walk you through exactly what happened, what we have done about it, and what we are putting in place to prevent it from happening again."

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Scenario: Budget Review or Cost Pressure" defaultOpen={false}>

**Signals:** Customer mentions budget cuts, asks for pricing review, finance team joins the QBR, procurement asks for competitive bids.

**Approach:**
1. **Shift from cost to value.** Reframe the conversation: "Let us look at the return you are getting on this investment before we discuss the investment itself"
2. **Quantify ROI relentlessly.** Show specific dollar values: revenue generated, time saved, campaigns enabled, headcount avoided
3. **Present tier options.** If a downgrade is necessary, present it as an option alongside the recommendation to maintain current investment
4. **Introduce multi-year incentives.** Offer a multi-year commitment in exchange for improved pricing, but only with sales alignment
5. **Never negotiate live in a QBR.** If pricing comes up, say: "I want to give this the attention it deserves. Let me take this back to our team and schedule a focused conversation this week"

**Key phrase:** "I understand budget pressure is real. Let me make sure we have a clear picture of the value being delivered so we can make the best decision together."

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Scenario: Competitive Threat" defaultOpen={false}>

**Signals:** Customer mentions other CDPs, asks about specific competitor features, shares an RFP, or requests a feature comparison.

**Approach:**
1. **Do not panic or bash competitors.** Stay confident and professional
2. **Ask what is driving the evaluation.** Is it a genuine gap, a procurement requirement, or an internal champion for another vendor?
3. **Focus on switching costs.** Highlight the integrations, data pipelines, identity graphs, and team training that are already in place
4. **Address gaps honestly.** If a competitor has a feature you lack, acknowledge it and share the roadmap timeline if applicable
5. **Activate your executive sponsor.** Request an exec-to-exec conversation to reinforce the strategic partnership
6. **Engage your competitive intelligence team internally.** Get the latest battlecards and win/loss data

**Key phrase:** "We welcome the comparison. Let us make sure any evaluation includes the full picture: the integrations already built, the team expertise developed, and the roadmap ahead."

</Expandable>

<Callout type="important" title="Difficult QBRs Require Pre-Alignment">
Never walk into a difficult QBR without first aligning with your champion. A 15-minute pre-call to understand the room dynamics, who is driving the concern, and what outcome the customer is looking for will prevent surprises and help you tailor your approach. If your champion is unwilling to pre-align, that itself is a red flag worth noting.
</Callout>

A fast, imperfect follow-up beats a slow, polished one.

### QBR Formats by Purpose

Not all QBRs should follow the same format. Tailor the structure to the audience, the account situation, and the strategic objective.

<Expandable title="Format 1: Standard QBR (60 minutes)" defaultOpen={true}>

**When to use:** Regular quarterly cadence for healthy or at-risk accounts. The default format.

**Audience:** Champion, key stakeholders, executive sponsor (optional but encouraged).

**Focus areas:** Balanced review across all six agenda sections. Equal weight to retrospective and forward-looking content.

**Preparation effort:** Standard (2-week prep cycle).

**Agenda:** Follow the 60-minute template outlined above.

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Format 2: Executive Sponsor QBR (45 minutes)" defaultOpen={false}>

**When to use:** When the executive sponsor has confirmed attendance or when you need to re-engage a disengaged sponsor. Shorter format respects executive time.

**Audience:** Executive sponsor, VP/Director-level Zeotap leader, champion (for context).

**Focus areas:** Business outcomes and strategic alignment only. Skip granular platform metrics. Lead with the executive summary and spend most of the time on strategic recommendations and partnership vision.

**Preparation effort:** High. Requires Zeotap executive briefing and custom talking points.

**Agenda:**
- 0:00-0:05 — Introductions and context setting
- 0:05-0:15 — Executive summary and business impact review
- 0:15-0:25 — Strategic partnership vision and roadmap highlights
- 0:25-0:35 — Customer priorities and alignment discussion
- 0:35-0:42 — Next quarter strategic objectives
- 0:42-0:45 — Wrap-up and commitments

<Callout type="tip" title="Executive Sponsor Engagement Rule">
Never present more than 5 slides to an executive sponsor. Use a conversational format with a one-page summary as a leave-behind. Executives want dialogue, not a presentation. Prepare your CSM or champion to handle any detailed questions that arise by having the full deck available as backup.
</Callout>

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Format 3: Technical Deep Dive QBR (90 minutes)" defaultOpen={false}>

**When to use:** When the account has significant technical complexity, is implementing new integrations, or has unresolved technical issues. Often run mid-quarter as a supplement to the standard QBR.

**Audience:** Customer data engineering team, solutions architect, Zeotap solutions consultant, CSM.

**Focus areas:** Data pipeline health, integration architecture, identity resolution configuration, destination sync performance, API usage, and technical roadmap.

**Preparation effort:** High. Requires solutions consultant involvement and technical data pull.

**Agenda:**
- 0:00-0:05 — Objectives and scope
- 0:05-0:20 — Data ingestion architecture review (volumes, latency, error rates)
- 0:20-0:35 — Identity resolution deep dive (match rates by source, graph quality, merge rules)
- 0:35-0:50 — Destination sync performance (success rates, latency, error patterns by destination)
- 0:50-1:05 — Ada AI and API usage patterns
- 1:05-1:20 — Technical roadmap and upcoming platform changes
- 1:20-1:30 — Action items and technical priorities for next quarter

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Format 4: Renewal-Focused QBR (75 minutes)" defaultOpen={false}>

**When to use:** When the account's renewal is within 120 days. This format blends value reinforcement with renewal positioning.

**Audience:** Champion, executive sponsor, procurement/finance (if invited), Zeotap sales lead, CSM.

**Focus areas:** Cumulative ROI over the contract period, total value delivered, forward-looking business case, and partnership continuation plan.

**Preparation effort:** Very high. Requires sales alignment, pricing review, and potentially custom ROI analysis.

**Agenda:**
- 0:00-0:05 — Welcome and partnership timeline recap
- 0:05-0:20 — Cumulative business outcomes: value delivered over the full contract period
- 0:20-0:30 — Platform adoption maturity: where they started vs. where they are now
- 0:30-0:40 — Forward-looking business case: what additional value can be unlocked in the next contract period
- 0:40-0:50 — Product roadmap alignment: what is coming that reinforces their investment
- 0:50-1:00 — Next steps on renewal: timeline, process, key decisions
- 1:00-1:10 — Customer feedback and open discussion
- 1:10-1:15 — Commitments and follow-up

<Callout type="important" title="Renewal QBR Coordination with Sales">
The renewal-focused QBR must be jointly planned with your sales counterpart. Agree in advance on: who presents the commercial section, what pricing or packaging options to reference, and how to handle objections. Misalignment between CS and Sales in front of a customer is one of the fastest ways to undermine trust.
</Callout>

</Expandable>

### QBR Anti-Patterns to Avoid

<Expandable title="Anti-Pattern 1: The Data Dump" defaultOpen={false}>

**What it looks like:** 30 slides of charts and metrics with no narrative. The CSM reads numbers off the screen while the customer checks their phone.

**Why it fails:** Data without interpretation is noise. Executives do not want to do the analysis themselves. They want your perspective on what the data means.

**Fix:** For every data point, answer "so what?" If you cannot explain why a metric matters, remove the slide.

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Anti-Pattern 2: The Product Demo" defaultOpen={false}>

**What it looks like:** The CSM spends 20 minutes demoing new features that the customer did not ask about. The QBR becomes a sales pitch.

**Why it fails:** QBRs are about the customer's business, not your product. Feature demos belong in separate enablement sessions.

**Fix:** Limit product content to the roadmap section. Only highlight features that directly map to the customer's stated priorities.

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Anti-Pattern 3: The Surprise Attack" defaultOpen={false}>

**What it looks like:** The CSM brings up expansion, renewal pricing, or a contract change without pre-aligning with the champion or sales team.

**Why it fails:** It erodes trust and puts the champion in an awkward position in front of their leadership.

**Fix:** Always pre-align commercial topics with your champion and sales counterpart. The QBR should confirm what has already been discussed, not introduce new commercial surprises.

</Expandable>

<Expandable title="Anti-Pattern 4: The Monologue" defaultOpen={false}>

**What it looks like:** The CSM talks for 55 of the 60 minutes. The customer barely speaks.

**Why it fails:** A QBR without customer dialogue is a missed opportunity. You learn nothing, and the customer feels talked at, not engaged.

**Fix:** Target a 60/40 split: 60% CSM presentation, 40% customer discussion. Build discussion prompts into every section. Pause after each section and ask: "Does this match your experience? What are we missing?"

</Expandable>
